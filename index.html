Threat Modeling Quick Reference Guide

A practical guide to getting started with threat modeling at BigBox Co.

The Four Key Questions

1. What are we trying to protect? (Assets)

  • Customer data
  • Financial information
  • Intellectual property
  • Business operations
  • Staff information
  • System access

2. Who might want it? (Threat Actors)

Script Kiddies

Low capability, motivated by curiosity/bragging rights. Uses pre-built tools.

Hacktivists

Medium capability, motivated by causes. Wants to make statements.

Cyber Criminals

High capability, motivated by money. Systematic and persistent.

Cyber Terrorists

High capability, motivated by ideology. Want to spread fear, uncertainty and doubt

Insider Threats

High access, various motivations. Could be malicious or accidental.

Nation States

Highest capability, political motives. Sophisticated and well-resourced.

3. How might they try to get it? (Attack Methods)

Social Engineering

  • Phishing
  • Impersonation
  • Social media research

Technical Attacks

  • Exploiting vulnerabilities
  • Password attacks
  • Malware

Physical Access

  • Unauthorised entry
  • Device theft
  • Dumpster diving

4. What would happen if they succeeded? (Impact)

  • Financial loss
  • Reputational damage
  • Operational disruption
  • Legal/regulatory consequences
  • Customer harm

Getting Started

1. Pick One Asset

  • Start small
  • Choose something important
  • Consider dependencies

2. Ask These Questions

  • Where is it stored?
  • Who has access?
  • How is it protected?
  • What protections exist?

Common Attack Paths to Consider

  • Compromised credentials
  • Unpatched systems
  • Misconfigured access
  • Third-party vulnerabilities
  • Human error
  • Physical access
  • Social engineering

Quick Tips

  • Think like an attacker
  • Consider both technical and non-technical threats
  • Document your findings
  • Start simple and build complexity
  • Focus on practical mitigations
